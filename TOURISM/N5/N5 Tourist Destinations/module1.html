Unit 1.1: Location

Namibia is situated in the **southwestern part of Africa**. It is:

  • North: Bordered by Angola and Zambia
  • East: Bordered by Botswana
  • South: Bordered by South Africa
  • West: Bordered by the Atlantic Ocean

Its **coastline stretches for over 1,500 km** along the Atlantic Ocean, making it a key entry point for maritime trade and tourism in Southern Africa. Namibia is characterized by vast desert landscapes, rugged mountains, and a rich diversity of flora and fauna. The country is well known for the Namib and Kalahari Deserts, which contribute to its unique ecosystem and tourist appeal.

Unit 1.2: Airport Cities, Main Tourist Centres and Transport Routes

  • Accessibility from South Africa:
    • By Air: Regular direct flights from Johannesburg (O.R. Tambo International Airport) and Cape Town International Airport land at Hosea Kutako International Airport near Windhoek. Air Namibia (now defunct) used to operate these routes; regional airlines such as FlyNamibia and South African Airways now serve this route.
    • By Road: Namibia is accessible via well-maintained national roads. Common border posts include:
      • Vioolsdrif–Noordoewer (on the N7/B1 route between South Africa and Namibia)
      • Nakop–Ariamsvlei (via Upington to Keetmanshoop)
      • Ramatlabama–Trans Kalahari Border (via Botswana into Namibia)
    • By Rail: Limited railway services exist between Namibia and South Africa via TransNamib. These are mainly for cargo; passenger services are minimal and not frequently used by tourists.
    • By Sea: The Port of Walvis Bay is Namibia’s main deep-sea harbour. Cruise liners and commercial vessels often dock here. The port also serves as a gateway to Swakopmund and the Skeleton Coast.
  • Main Airport Cities:
    • Windhoek – Hosea Kutako International Airport (WDH)
    • Walvis Bay – Walvis Bay International Airport (WVB)
    • Eros Airport – Located within Windhoek, used for regional and domestic flights
  • Main Tourist Centres:
    • Etosha National Park – One of Africa's top wildlife viewing parks, home to elephants, lions, rhinos, giraffes, and more
    • Swakopmund – Coastal resort town known for German colonial architecture, adventure sports, and beaches
    • Sossusvlei – Famous for red sand dunes and the surreal Dead Vlei pan in the Namib Desert
    • Skeleton Coast – Remote coastal area known for shipwrecks, desert landscapes, and seal colonies
    • Fish River Canyon – Second largest canyon in the world, popular for hiking and scenic views
  • Principal Transport Routes:
    • Trans-Kalahari Highway: Connects Namibia with Botswana and South Africa; major east-west trade and travel corridor
    • B1 Highway: Runs north-south from the Angolan border through Windhoek to the South African border
    • B2 Highway: Links the coast (Swakopmund/Walvis Bay) to Windhoek
    • TransNamib Railway: Provides freight and limited passenger rail services connecting key cities

Unit 1.4: Form of Government

Namibia is a **sovereign democratic republic** with a **multi-party system**, established after its independence from South African administration on **21 March 1990**.

The country follows a **constitutional democracy**, where the **President of Namibia** serves as both **Head of State** and **Head of Government**. The executive power is vested in the President, who is elected by direct popular vote for a **five-year term**, renewable once.

The Namibian government is structured into **three branches**:

  • Executive: Led by the President, who appoints a Prime Minister and Cabinet Ministers to oversee various departments.
  • Legislative: Consists of the National Assembly (lower house) and the National Council (upper house). These bodies are responsible for debating and passing laws.
  • Judiciary: Independent courts, headed by the Supreme Court, interpret and apply laws in accordance with the Constitution.

Namibia's Constitution is considered one of the most progressive in Africa, guaranteeing **fundamental human rights**, **freedom of speech**, **gender equality**, and **protection of the environment**.

General elections take place every five years. Citizens elect:

  • The President
  • Members of the National Assembly
  • Regional Councils and Local Authorities

Namibia is a member of international organisations such as the **United Nations (UN)**, the **African Union (AU)**, and the **Southern African Development Community (SADC)**, promoting peace, development, and regional integration.

Exam Tip:

For exam purposes, remember that Namibia is a **democratic republic** with a **multi-party system** and an **executive president**. You may be asked to name the **three branches of government**, explain how leaders are elected, or identify the roles of the **National Assembly** and **National Council**. Also, be familiar with **election frequency**, **key democratic values**, and Namibia’s **constitutional guarantees** such as **freedom, equality, and justice**.

Unit 1.5: Practical Information

  • Currency: The official currency is the Namibian Dollar (NAD), which is pegged 1:1 to the South African Rand (ZAR). Both currencies are accepted in Namibia.
  • Entry Requirements: South African citizens do not require a visa for visits up to 90 days. A valid passport is essential. Visitors from other countries should check visa requirements with Namibian embassies.
  • Time Zone: Namibia operates on Central Africa Time (CAT), which is UTC +2 — the same as South Africa.
  • Official Language: English is the official language. However, other widely spoken languages include:
    • Oshiwambo (most spoken native language)
    • Afrikaans
    • German
    • Herero, Nama, Damara, Rukwangali, Lozi, and Setswana
  • Electricity: Voltage is 220V AC. Plug types used are Type D (3 round pins in a triangle) and Type M (similar to South African plugs).
  • Public Holidays: Namibia observes the following national holidays:

Unit 1.6: Climate Conditions

  • Tropic of Capricorn: The Tropic of Capricorn passes through the central region of Namibia, influencing its hot and arid climate. Namibia lies mostly within the subtropical high-pressure belt, which results in predominantly dry weather conditions.
  • General Climate: Namibia has a combination of desert, semi-desert, and savanna climates. It experiences low and unpredictable rainfall, intense sunshine (over 300 sunny days per year), and wide temperature ranges between day and night.
  • Rainfall: The rainy season occurs mainly between November and April. However, rainfall varies across the country:
    • North and northeast regions (e.g., Caprivi/Zambezi) receive the most rainfall (up to 600 mm annually).
    • Southern and western parts (e.g., Namib Desert) receive the least (as low as 25 mm annually).
  • Temperature:
    • Summer (October – April): Hot and sometimes humid in the north; temperatures can exceed 35°C in desert areas.
    • Winter (May – September): Dry and cool; daytime temperatures are pleasant (18–25°C), but nights can drop below freezing in inland regions.
  • Desert Influence: The Namib Desert on the western coast creates cool mornings and fog due to the cold Benguela Current, while the Kalahari Desert in the east causes dry inland conditions.
  • Best Time to Visit: The ideal time to travel to Namibia is during the . During this period, there is:
    • Little to no rainfall
    • Clear skies and comfortable temperatures
    • Excellent wildlife viewing, as animals gather at waterholes

Exam Tip:

You may be asked to describe the **overall climate of Namibia**, **identify seasonal travel periods**, or explain the impact of **desert and coastal geography on climate**. Make sure you know:

  • Which season is the best for tourism (May to October)
  • The effect of the **Tropic of Capricorn** on temperature
  • The difference in rainfall between the **Namib** and **Zambezi** regions
Focus on how climate affects **tourism planning, clothing, and wildlife visibility**, which is essential for N5 assessments.

Unit 1.7: Economy and Agriculture

Namibia’s economy is classified as a developing and upper-middle-income economy. It is largely dependent on **primary industries** such as **mining**, **fishing**, and **agriculture**, as well as the **tourism sector**, which continues to grow due to the country's natural and cultural attractions.

1. Primary Economic Sector:

  • Mining: Namibia is rich in mineral resources, and mining is the leading contributor to GDP. The country is one of the world’s top producers of uranium and diamonds. Other minerals include copper, zinc, lead, gold, and fluorspar.
  • Fishing: Namibia’s Atlantic coast supports a strong fishing industry. Major exports include hake, pilchards, horse mackerel, and shellfish. Walvis Bay and Lüderitz serve as key fishing ports.
  • Agriculture: Agriculture plays a vital role in rural employment. Due to arid and semi-arid conditions, livestock farming dominates, especially cattle, sheep, and goats. Crop production is limited to areas with irrigation in the northern regions such as Kavango and Zambezi.

2. Secondary Economic Sector:

  • Includes **manufacturing** of meat products, fish processing, textiles, and construction materials. The sector is smaller but growing steadily through industrial development zones and value-added production.

3. Tertiary Economic Sector:

  • Tourism: A major source of foreign exchange and employment. Tourists visit attractions such as Etosha National Park, Sossusvlei, Fish River Canyon, and the Skeleton Coast. Namibia is known for eco-tourism and wildlife safaris.
  • Transport and Trade: Namibia serves as a transport hub for surrounding landlocked countries (Botswana, Zambia, Zimbabwe). The Walvis Bay Corridor connects Namibia to regional and international trade routes.
  • Banking and Services: A growing services sector supports financial services, education, healthcare, and public administration.

Exam Tip:

You may be asked to explain the contribution of different sectors of the economy to Namibia’s development. Learn to differentiate between primary, secondary, and tertiary sectors. Focus especially on **mining (diamonds & uranium)**, **livestock farming**, and the role of **tourism** in promoting sustainable development. Also, be able to explain why **crop farming is limited** and in which regions it is successful.

Unit 1.8: Accessibility from South Africa

By Air: Namibia’s national airline, formerly known as Air Namibia (SW), operated direct international flights from South Africa. Currently, flights are available through other carriers such as FlyNamibia, South African Airways (SAA), and Airlink. These flights connect major South African cities like Johannesburg (OR Tambo International) and Cape Town to Hosea Kutako International Airport (WDH), located about 45 km east of Windhoek. The flight duration is approximately 2 hours. A shuttle or taxi takes 35–45 minutes from the airport to Windhoek city centre.

  • Ariamsvlei–Nakop (B3): Connects to Keetmanshoop and central Namibia.

Unit 1.11: Shopping in Namibia

Namibia provides a wide variety of authentic and culturally rich shopping experiences, especially appealing to tourists looking for souvenirs and traditional art. Many of the products reflect the country’s diverse cultures and are handmade by local artisans using natural materials such as wood, leather, beads, and textiles.

In the capital city, Windhoek, one of the most popular locations is the Namibian Craft Centre, located in the Old Breweries Complex. It houses over 40 stalls and cooperatives representing artists and communities from across the country. Popular items available include:

  • Herero Dolls: Dressed in traditional Herero attire, symbolising Namibian cultural heritage.
  • Hand-carved Wooden Sculptures: Often featuring wildlife, tribal faces, and traditional scenes.
  • Beaded Jewellery and Accessories: Using vibrant colours and African patterns.
  • Leather Goods: Including bags, sandals, belts, and wallets made from game hides.
  • Textiles: Traditional fabrics and hand-dyed wraps inspired by local patterns.
  • Gems and Semi-Precious Stones: Namibia is a known source of stones like rose quartz, amethyst, and tourmaline.
  • Handcrafted Pottery and Woven Baskets: Often made by rural women’s cooperatives.

Regional Shopping Highlights

  • Keetmanshoop – Karas Huisen Crafts: Offers locally made leather goods, carved wooden bowls, beaded jewelry, and textiles reflecting Southern Namibian culture.
  • Okahandja – Vyf Rand Kamp Market: One of Namibia’s largest open-air craft markets, popular for woodcarvings, masks, figurines, and woven crafts.
  • Rundu – Mbungura Woodcraft Cooperative: Located in the Kavango Region, this group produces hand-carved statues, wildlife art, and traditional house decorations.
  • Caprivi/Zambezi Region: Known for colourful woven baskets, reed mats, and tribal jewelry made from natural and recycled materials. Many items are sold in roadside markets or village stalls.
  • Swakopmund: A popular coastal town with German colonial architecture, offering marine-themed crafts, desert stones, handmade soaps, and decorative shell jewelry.

Exam Tip:

When studying tourism shopping in Namibia, focus on the cultural significance of the crafts and their role in supporting local communities. Know key craft regions like Okahandja and Windhoek, and remember examples of items sold (e.g. Herero dolls, wood carvings, gemstones). You may be asked to identify popular shopping regions or match a craft product to a location. Also, note how tourism promotes local economies and preserves traditional arts.

Unit 1.12: Main Tourist Attractions

1.12.1 Windhoek, Surroundings and Central East

Natural Attractions

  • National Botanical Garden of Namibia (Windhoek): Located in central Windhoek, this botanical garden showcases Namibia's rich biodiversity. It is important for environmental education, indigenous plant conservation, and ecotourism. Visitors enjoy nature walks, birdwatching, and learning about drought-resistant flora.
  • Von Bach Recreation Resort (near Okahandja): Approximately 70 km north of Windhoek, this dam-based resort is popular for water sports, including fishing, sailing, swimming, and canoeing. The site also offers picnic spots and campgrounds, ideal for weekend getaways.
  • Gross-Barmen Hot Spring Resort (west of Okahandja): Known for its naturally heated pools and wellness spa, this resort promotes health tourism. The thermal spring water is rich in minerals believed to aid muscular and joint recovery. Accommodation is available for overnight visitors.
  • Hardap Game Reserve (near Mariental): Encompassing Namibia’s largest dam, this nature reserve offers game viewing (including antelope, black rhino, zebra, and ostriches), birdwatching, fishing, and boating. It supports both wildlife conservation and recreational tourism.
  • Khomas Hochland Mountains (Windhoek region): A rugged mountainous region forming part of the central plateau. The area offers scenic drives, hiking trails, and viewpoints overlooking Windhoek. It provides insight into Namibia’s unique topography and geological history.

Man-Made Attractions

  • Windhoek: The capital city combines modern development with historical significance. Key landmarks include:
    • Christuskirche: A historic Lutheran church built in 1907.
    • Independence Memorial Museum: Chronicles Namibia’s liberation history.
    • Parliament Gardens: Lush gardens ideal for relaxation and educational tours.
    • Post Street Mall: A pedestrian shopping area with craft vendors and cafes.
    Windhoek is the political, economic, and cultural heart of Namibia.
  • Okahandja: Just 70 km north of Windhoek, Okahandja is known for its famous craft market, featuring handcrafted woodwork and local souvenirs. The town hosts Herero Day annually in August, commemorating the history and traditions of the Herero people.
  • Gobabis: Situated 200 km east of Windhoek, this cattle-farming town is the gateway to Botswana. Gobabis is famous for its large agricultural shows and is a central trading hub in the Omaheke Region. It features a statue of a bull symbolizing its ranching identity.
  • Mariental: Located 260 km south of Windhoek, Mariental is a farming town near the Hardap River. It is known for irrigation-based agriculture including grapes and dates. It also acts as a base for visitors heading to the Hardap Game Reserve and Kalahari Desert.

Exam Tip:

For exam success, make sure you can clearly differentiate between natural and man-made attractions. Use full names and locations when identifying attractions (e.g., “Von Bach Dam, near Okahandja”), and understand why they are important to tourists (e.g., health, recreation, wildlife). You may also be asked to match a tourist town with its main attraction or explain the role of a town like Windhoek in national tourism.

1.12.2 Etosha and the Land of the Owambo People

Natural Attractions

  • Etosha National Park: Situated in northern Namibia, approximately 435 km north of Windhoek, Etosha is one of the largest and most iconic game parks in Africa. The name "Etosha" means "Great White Place," referencing the massive Etosha Pan—a salt pan that becomes a shallow lake in the rainy season and can be seen from space. The park is home to the Big Five (except buffalo), cheetahs, giraffes, hyenas, zebras, and over 300 species of birds, including flamingos and secretary birds. Etosha is essential for eco-tourism, conservation, and education, often cited in exams for its biodiversity and conservation efforts.
  • Lake Otjikoto: Located near the town of Tsumeb, Lake Otjikoto is Namibia’s only permanent natural lake. It was formed by the collapse of a limestone cave, resulting in a sinkhole lake with a mysterious, emerald-green appearance. During World War I, retreating German forces dumped weapons into the lake, making it not only a geological wonder but also a historic site. The lake is popular among divers and historians and is frequently referenced in tourism assessments.

Man-Made Attractions

  • Outjo: Located about 310 km north of Windhoek, Outjo is often referred to as the southern gateway to Etosha. The town provides a range of services to tourists, including lodges, guesthouses, shops, and restaurants. Its location makes it a convenient base for safari tourists heading into the Etosha National Park through the Anderson Gate.
  • Tsumeb: Roughly 435 km from Windhoek and closest to the Von Lindequist Gate on the eastern side of Etosha, Tsumeb is historically significant due to its mining industry. It was a major center for copper, lead, and zinc mining. The Tsumeb Museum offers valuable insights into Namibia’s mining history, German colonial heritage, and local culture. It is also a stopover for tourists travelling between Etosha and the Caprivi/Zambezi region.
  • Oshakati and Ondangwa: These major towns are located in the north-central part of Namibia, in the Owambo (or Wambo) region. Oshakati is a modern administrative and commercial hub, known for its open markets, shopping malls, and transport links. Ondangwa, with roots as a Finnish missionary station, is a cultural center rich in local traditions. Both towns are central to understanding the Owambo people—Namibia’s largest ethnic group—and their role in the country’s history, especially during the independence struggle. These areas also serve as access points to cultural tourism routes in the north.

Exam Tip:

You may be asked to name both natural and man-made attractions in the Etosha region, or to identify the cultural significance of the Owambo towns. Pay close attention to locations and spelling (e.g., Tsumeb, Ondangwa) and remember to link towns like Outjo or Oshakati to their tourism functions. For bonus marks, mention that Etosha is important for both wildlife conservation and international tourism, and that Lake Otjikoto has geological and historical value.

1.12.3 Kaokoland, the Kunene River and the Himba

Natural Attractions

  • Desert-Adapted Elephants of Kaokoland: Located in Namibia’s remote northwestern region, these elephants are uniquely adapted to the harsh, arid desert environment. Unlike their savanna relatives, they have smaller bodies, longer legs, and travel long distances in search of water and food. Their existence highlights conservation success in Namibia and draws eco-tourists interested in rare desert wildlife.
  • Epupa Falls: Found on the Kunene River near the town of Epupa, these cascading waterfalls span about 1.5 km and drop roughly 37 meters. Surrounded by baobab trees and rocky terrain, Epupa Falls is culturally significant to the Himba people and offers opportunities for photography, hiking, birdwatching, and cultural interaction. It’s a popular destination for those interested in remote natural beauty and indigenous heritage.
  • Ruacana Falls: Located on the Kunene River near Ruacana, close to the Angolan border, these falls are one of the largest in Namibia, particularly spectacular during the rainy season (January–April). Besides being a scenic attraction, the site also houses a hydroelectric power station, providing energy to the region. Tourists are attracted by both the natural power of the falls and their engineering significance.
  • Kunene River: This river forms the natural northern boundary between Namibia and Angola. It originates in Angola’s central highlands and flows westward into the Atlantic Ocean. It supports agriculture, fishing, and hydroelectric power generation. The river's banks are also a rich ecological zone, supporting birds, crocodiles, and hippos, and are integral to local communities and tourism.

Man-Made Attraction

  • Himba Villages and Cultural Experience: The Himba are one of Namibia’s most iconic ethnic groups, known for their red-ochre skin, traditional hairstyles, and jewelry made from leather, shells, and metal. Most Himba settlements are found in the Kunene Region near Opuwo, approximately 720 km northwest of Windhoek. Tourists can visit these villages through guided tours to learn about their pastoral lifestyle, customs, spiritual beliefs, and indigenous knowledge systems. This promotes cultural preservation and offers income opportunities for the communities.

Exam Tip:

When answering exam questions about Kaokoland, always link natural attractions (like the Kunene River and Epupa Falls) with cultural significance (such as the Himba people). Be able to describe the unique adaptation of desert elephants and highlight the importance of water sources like the Kunene River in this semi-arid region. Don’t forget to include the role of tourism in both environmental conservation and cultural sustainability.

1.12.4 Khaudum, Kavango and Zambezi Region

Natural Attractions

  • Okavango River: Originating in the Angolan highlands, the Okavango River flows through the Kavango Region of Namibia before reaching Botswana, where it forms the Okavango Delta. In Namibia, it supports local communities, wildlife, and agriculture. Tourists enjoy river cruises, cultural visits, and fishing, especially around Divundu and Rundu.
  • Khaudum National Park: Situated in the northeastern wilderness near the Botswana border, Khaudum is known for its untamed, remote character. The park is rich in biodiversity, home to elephants, lions, hyenas, leopards, African wild dogs, and over 300 species of birds. It is ideal for experienced 4x4 adventurers seeking solitude and raw nature.
  • Mangetti National Park: Located in the eastern Oshikoto Region, this park promotes community-based conservation and eco-tourism. It features savannah woodland landscapes and is home to giraffes, kudu, and various bird species. Mangetti also supports local livelihoods through tourism and employment.
  • Bwabwata National Park: Spanning both Kavango and Zambezi regions, this unique park is a core part of the Kavango-Zambezi Transfrontier Conservation Area (KAZA TFCA). It merges human settlement with conservation and features elephants, buffalo, antelope, and birdlife. The park also preserves ancient migration corridors.
  • Mudumu National Park: Located along the Kwando River in the Zambezi Region, this park has riverine forests, mopane woodlands, and wetlands. Tourists can see elephants, crocodiles, hippos, sitatunga, and numerous bird species. It supports both camping and guided game viewing.
  • Nkasa Rupara National Park (formerly Mamili): This is Namibia’s largest wetland conservation area. Often compared to the Okavango Delta, it features swamps, reed beds, floodplains, and river channels. Tourists enjoy canoe safaris, birdwatching, and remote wildlife experiences. The park plays a critical role in the preservation of water ecosystems.
  • Popa Game Park: A small protected area surrounding Popa Falls near Divundu. It provides excellent opportunities for birdwatching and riverside relaxation. The falls are scenic rather than tall and are a popular picnic spot among locals and visitors.

Man-Made Attractions

  • Katima Mulilo: The administrative and economic hub of the Zambezi Region, located on the Zambezi River, near Zambia and Botswana. It is a strategic cross-border town offering access to Victoria Falls, Livingstone, and Chobe National Park. It also hosts markets, government buildings, and regional transport services.
  • Rundu: A major town in Kavango East on the Okavango River. Known for its woodcarving industry, local crafts, and cultural centers. Rundu serves as a vital trade route and administrative center for northern Namibia. It also has museums and hosts traditional dance festivals.

Exam Tip:

In exams, be able to distinguish the parks in the Kavango and Zambezi regions by their unique characteristics—such as Khaudum’s remoteness, Bwabwata’s co-managed conservation model, and Nkasa Rupara’s wetlands. Mention cross-border tourism, the role of rivers like the Okavango and Zambezi, and how tourism supports both the economy and conservation. Knowing key towns like Rundu and Katima Mulilo and their gateway roles is also essential.

1.12.5 Waterberg and Surroundings

Natural Attractions

  • Waterberg Plateau Park: Located approximately 280 km northeast of Windhoek near Otjiwarongo, this park sits atop a massive red sandstone plateau rising 200 meters above the surrounding savannah. The park is home to endangered species like the black rhino, roan antelope, and sable antelope. Visitors can explore hiking trails, enjoy guided wildlife drives, and participate in conservation education programs. The park is a key example of Namibia’s conservation success and is often studied in ecotourism topics.
  • Hoba Meteorite: Situated on the Hoba West Farm, 24 km west of Grootfontein, this is the largest known intact meteorite on Earth, weighing around 60 tons. Composed mainly of iron and nickel, it has remained at its original landing site. The meteorite attracts geologists, students, and tourists interested in astronomy and earth sciences.
  • Dragon's Breath Cave: Near Grootfontein, this cave holds the world’s largest underground non-subglacial lake. Named after the humid air rising from its entrance, the cave is a global speleological site, although not generally open to the public. It is of scientific and ecological interest and often referenced in geographical and geological studies.

Man-Made Attractions

  • The Living Museum of the Ju/'Hoansi-San at Grashoek: Located northeast of Grootfontein, this cultural site showcases the ancient traditions of the Ju/'Hoansi-San (Bushmen) people, including hunting, fire-making, dancing, and storytelling. The museum promotes cultural preservation and community-based tourism. It serves as an educational platform for both locals and international tourists to learn about Namibia’s indigenous heritage.
  • Grootfontein: This town in the Otjozondjupa Region lies about 450 km northeast of Windhoek. It features colonial German architecture and acts as a central hub for visiting the Hoba Meteorite and surrounding attractions. The town also contains museums, shops, and agricultural research stations.
  • Omaruru: Located roughly 230 km northwest of Windhoek, Omaruru is a vibrant artistic town known for its craft breweries, artisan chocolate, and art galleries. It’s a hotspot for local entrepreneurs and is celebrated for its cultural festivals and creative tourism. Omaruru promotes Namibian art and innovation.

Exam Tip:

In the exam, be able to differentiate clearly between natural and man-made attractions in this region. Know the significance of Waterberg Plateau Park in conservation, and why the Hoba Meteorite is geologically important. Cultural tourism sites like the Living Museum of the Ju/'Hoansi-San often appear in questions relating to community-based tourism. Use location references (e.g. Otjiwarongo, Grootfontein, Grashoek) to show geographical understanding. Be ready to explain how eco-tourism supports both conservation and local livelihoods.

1.12.6 Twyfelfontein, Brandberg and Damaraland

Natural Attractions

  • Petrified Forest: Located near Khorixas in the Kunene Region, this forest features fossilized tree trunks that are estimated to be over 280 million years old. These trees were washed down by ancient rivers and buried by sediment, turning into stone over time. It is a globally recognized paleontological site and an important stop for geology enthusiasts.
  • Burnt Mountain: Found close to Twyfelfontein, this hill appears blackened or "burnt" due to a rare chemical reaction that altered its lava-rich rocks. The colors are especially vivid at sunrise or sunset. It is a striking natural phenomenon and a highlight for photographers and nature lovers.
  • Organ Pipes: These vertical dolerite rock columns resemble organ pipes and were formed through volcanic activity millions of years ago. Located near Burnt Mountain, this formation attracts geologists and is frequently included in educational excursions.
  • Doros Crater: A volcanic crater near Brandberg Mountain formed by ancient eruptions. It offers panoramic views of the surrounding terrain and is valued for its geological importance and scenic appeal.
  • Finger Rock: A prominent rock spire near Twyfelfontein that resembles an upright finger. It is a recognizable natural landmark and often used as a navigational reference in the region.
  • Brandberg Mountain: Namibia’s highest peak at 2,573 meters, located near the town of Uis in Damaraland. Brandberg is known for ancient San rock art, including the “White Lady” painting, which is believed to be over 2,000 years old. The site is sacred to local communities and offers demanding hiking routes.
  • Spitzkoppe: Sometimes called the “Matterhorn of Namibia,” these dramatic granite peaks rise sharply from the desert plains near Usakos. The area is known for its rock climbing, natural arches, and prehistoric rock paintings.

Man-Made Attractions

  • Twyfelfontein: A UNESCO World Heritage Site located about 100 km west of Outjo. Twyfelfontein features over 2,500 rock engravings and petroglyphs created by ancient San hunters and gatherers. The site is one of the most significant archaeological locations in Africa and provides insight into early human communication and spirituality.
  • Khorixas: A small town in the Kunene Region, about 400 km northwest of Windhoek. Khorixas serves as a regional service center for the surrounding tourist attractions, including the Petrified Forest and Twyfelfontein. It offers basic tourist facilities, fuel stops, and local markets.
  • Omaruru: Located about 230 km northwest of Windhoek, Omaruru is a culturally vibrant town famous for its artist communities, chocolate production, and craft breweries. It plays a significant role in Namibia’s cultural tourism and hosts annual art and music festivals.

Exam Tip:

For examination purposes, be able to list and distinguish between the key geological sites such as the Organ Pipes, Burnt Mountain, and Petrified Forest. Understand the cultural and archaeological importance of Twyfelfontein and the significance of Brandberg Mountain for both rock art and sacred heritage. You may be asked to identify which of these attractions are included in the UNESCO World Heritage list or to explain their educational and eco-tourism value.

1.12.7 Sossusvlei, the Namib-Naukluft Park and Environs

Natural Attractions

  • Sossusvlei (World Heritage Site): Situated in the southern region of the Namib Desert inside the Namib-Naukluft National Park, approximately 300 km southwest of Windhoek. Sossusvlei is a large, white salt and clay pan surrounded by some of the world’s highest sand dunes, reaching up to 325 meters. It is world-famous for its photogenic red dunes, stark landscapes, and unique desert-adapted wildlife such as oryx, springbok, and the Namib Desert beetle. It is a must-see site for eco-tourism and desert ecology studies.
  • Dune 45: Located 45 km from the Sesriem entrance gate, Dune 45 is the most climbed and photographed dune in the park. Its name comes from its distance from the gate, and it is prized for its accessible slope and stunning sunrise and sunset views due to the deep, changing dune colors.
  • Sesriem Canyon: Found 4 km from the Sesriem entrance, this canyon was carved over millions of years by the Tsauchab River. It is about 30 meters deep and offers insight into desert erosion processes. The name "Sesriem" means “six leather straps,” referring to how early settlers drew water from the canyon using six tied-together straps.
  • Namib-Naukluft National Park: Spanning nearly 50,000 km², this is one of Africa’s largest protected areas. It includes multiple ecosystems: gravel plains, towering dunes, rocky mountains, and dry riverbeds. The Namib is considered the world’s oldest desert, and the park supports unique flora and fauna, such as the welwitschia plant, Tok Tokkie beetles, and lizards that survive with minimal moisture.

Man-Made Attractions

  • Maltahöhe: A small rural village located around 300 km from Windhoek, acting as a gateway to the southern Namib region. It provides basic accommodation and services for visitors heading to Sossusvlei. It is often used as a base for desert tours.
  • Duwisib Castle: Positioned about 72 km from Maltahöhe, this unusual desert castle was built in 1908 by German baron Captain Hansheinrich von Wolf. The castle showcases European architecture, antique furniture, and thick fortress-like walls. It currently functions as a museum and historic monument, offering insight into colonial influence in Namibia.

Exam Tip:

For exams, make sure you can differentiate between natural and man-made attractions in this region. Know the unique environmental features of Sossusvlei, the role of Namib-Naukluft Park in conservation, and be able to locate Dune 45 and Sesriem Canyon on a map. You may also be asked to explain how desert tourism impacts conservation and rural development. Understand the significance of Duwisib Castle as a man-made attraction tied to colonial history.

1.12.8 Coastal Strip

Natural Attractions

  • Dorob National Park (including Welwitschia Mirabilis): Stretching along the central Namibian coast between the Kuiseb and Ugab Rivers, Dorob National Park protects a sensitive desert and marine ecosystem. It is home to the famous Welwitschia mirabilis plant, a rare species that can live for more than 1,000 years. This “living fossil” is adapted to arid environments. The park supports biodiversity, ecotourism, and recreational activities while preserving fragile desert habitats.
  • Walvis Bay Lagoon: One of the most important coastal wetlands in southern Africa, this lagoon is a designated Ramsar site. It supports thousands of migratory and resident bird species, including greater and lesser flamingos, pelicans, and avocets. The area is ideal for birdwatching, environmental education, and marine biology research.
  • Dune 7: Near Walvis Bay, Dune 7 is the highest dune in Namibia and one of the tallest in the world, reaching about 383 meters. It offers visitors the chance to climb its steep slopes for panoramic desert views. It is a key spot for sandboarding, photography, and adventure tourism.
  • Cape Cross Nature Reserve: Located approximately 120 km north of Swakopmund, Cape Cross is home to one of the world’s largest colonies of Cape fur seals. The site is also historically significant for Portuguese exploration—navigator Diego Cão planted a cross here in 1486. It is popular for ecotourism and marine education.

Man-Made Attractions

  • Swakopmund: A major coastal town located about 350 km west of Windhoek, Swakopmund is known for its German colonial-style buildings, seaside promenade, and vibrant tourism industry. It offers adventure sports such as skydiving, quad biking, and desert tours. Swakopmund is also a cultural and historical center, with museums and art galleries.
  • Walvis Bay: Situated 30 km south of Swakopmund, Walvis Bay is Namibia’s primary deep-water port and a hub for shipping, fishing, and tourism. Tourists enjoy boat cruises to see dolphins, whales, seals, and marine birds. The town also offers kayaking, windsurfing, and seafood experiences.
  • Henties Bay: About 120 km north of Swakopmund, Henties Bay is a quiet coastal town favored for recreational angling, off-road driving, and birdwatching. It provides access to the Skeleton Coast and is ideal for nature lovers seeking a more relaxed beach environment.

Exam Tip:

In your exam, be able to identify natural and man-made attractions along Namibia's Coastal Strip. Be familiar with Dorob National Park and Walvis Bay Lagoon for their biodiversity significance. Know the differences between Swakopmund, Walvis Bay, and Henties Bay in terms of tourism offerings. Also, remember that Cape Cross is important for both marine wildlife and historical relevance. Diagrams, maps, or short notes may be required on these.

1.12.8 Fish River, Coastal and the Deep South

Natural Attractions

  • Fish River Canyon: Located in southern Namibia near the town of Hobas, the Fish River Canyon is the second largest canyon in the world, stretching over 160 km in length and up to 550 meters deep. It is renowned for its dramatic landscapes and challenging hiking trails, attracting nature lovers and adventure tourists. The canyon is significant for its geological formations and unique desert ecosystem.
  • /Ai-/Ais Richtersveld Transfrontier Park: This transboundary park combines Namibia’s /Ai-/Ais Hot Springs Game Park with South Africa’s Richtersveld National Park. It is important for conservation of desert-adapted wildlife and its therapeutic hot springs. The park offers eco-tourism activities like hiking, wildlife viewing, and hot spring relaxation.
  • Orange River: Forming the southern border between Namibia and South Africa, the Orange River is vital for irrigation and agriculture in the region. It also offers water sports opportunities such as rafting and fishing, and supports diverse birdlife and aquatic species.
  • The Desert Horses of the Namib: Found near the town of Garub, these wild horses are uniquely adapted to the harsh desert environment. They are a major tourist attraction and symbolize the resilience of wildlife in extreme conditions.
  • Sperrgebiet National Park: Once a restricted diamond mining area along the Namibian coast, Sperrgebiet is now a protected national park with limited access. It hosts unique desert flora and fauna, many endemic species, and offers specialized eco-tourism experiences.
  • Mesosaurus Fossil Site: Located near Grootfontein, this paleontological site provides important evidence supporting the theory of continental drift, through fossils of the ancient reptile Mesosaurus. It is valuable for educational tourism and scientific study.
  • Brukkaros Mountain: A volcanic mountain in southern Namibia known for its distinctive crater and expansive panoramic views. It is a geological landmark and attracts hikers and nature enthusiasts.
  • Quiver Tree Forest: Situated near Keetmanshoop, this forest contains the largest concentration of quiver trees (Aloe dichotoma), an iconic succulent species in Namibia. The forest is culturally significant to indigenous communities and popular with tourists for photography and nature walks.
  • Giant's Playground: A geological formation near Keetmanshoop consisting of massive dolerite boulders piled upon each other. It is a scenic spot for hiking and photography, illustrating fascinating rock formations.

Man-Made Attractions

  • Namib-Naukluft Park Visitor Facilities: This area offers important infrastructure supporting tourism to the Namib Desert, including accommodation, guided tours, and educational centers.
  • Kolmanskop: A ghost town near Lüderitz, famous for its diamond mining history and sand-filled buildings. Kolmanskop is a popular site for guided tours showcasing Namibia’s mining heritage and the effects of desert encroachment.
  • Oranjemund: A diamond mining town located at the mouth of the Orange River, Oranjemund plays a key role in Namibia’s mining industry. Tourism here is controlled, with guided visits that highlight the mining lifestyle and history.
  • Keetmanshoop: The administrative center of the Karas Region, known for being the gateway to southern Namibia’s attractions such as the Quiver Tree Forest and Giant’s Playground. It offers accommodation and serves as a base for tourists exploring the deep south.

Exam Tip:

Be sure to familiarize yourself with the major natural and man-made attractions of Namibia’s deep south and coastal areas. Understand the ecological importance of places like Fish River Canyon and Sperrgebiet National Park, as well as the cultural and historical significance of towns like Kolmanskop and Oranjemund. You may be asked to explain conservation efforts, tourism activities, and the unique features of these sites in your exam.
