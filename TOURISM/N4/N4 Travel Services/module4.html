Unit 2: Travel Manuals

2.1 Code and decode city, airport, and airline codes using official manuals and references. Travel manuals such as the IATA Airline Coding Directory provide detailed lists of standardized codes essential for reservations and ticketing.

2.2 Reading and Interpreting Information from Travel Manuals

  • 2.2.1 Government Gazette of South Africa (GSA): The GSA publishes official regulations and notices relating to travel, transport, and tourism that consultants need to understand and interpret.
  • 2.2.2 Domestic and International Flight Timetables: These manuals provide schedules for flights, including departure and arrival times, airlines operating routes, and seasonal changes.
  • 2.2.3 National Accommodation Association of South Africa (NAA-SA): A manual containing details on accredited accommodation providers, useful for making reservations and advising clients.
  • 2.2.4 Rail Transport Manuals: Include schedules, routes, and tariffs for national and regional rail services, important for integrated travel planning.
  • 2.2.5 Motor Coach Travel Manuals: Contain information on coach operators, routes, stops, and ticketing procedures for road travel.

Unit 3: Minimum Connecting Time (MCT)

3.1 Meanings of Terms Relating to MCT

  • 3.1.1 Online: Flights operated by the same airline or within the same airline alliance, where connection times are usually shorter due to coordinated scheduling.
  • 3.1.2 Interline: Flights operated by different airlines that have agreements to handle passenger transfers and baggage connections.
  • 3.1.3 Dom-Dom (Domestic to Domestic): Connecting flights within the same country.
  • 3.1.4 Dom-Int or Int-Dom (Domestic to International or vice versa): Connecting flights between domestic and international terminals.
  • 3.1.5 Int-Int (International to International): Connecting flights between international terminals or airports.

3.2 Using Terms to Determine MCT at Airports Using the Official Airline Guide (OAG)

The Official Airline Guide (OAG) provides published minimum connecting times for different airports based on the flight types and terminals involved. Travel consultants use this to advise passengers on sufficient transfer time to avoid missed connections.

üìò Exam Tip

Understand the definitions and differences between terms like online, interline, Dom-Dom, and Int-Int. Be able to apply these when calculating minimum connecting times from provided timetables or guides such as the OAG. Exam questions may include scenario-based problems on transfer planning.

4.1 Calculating and Quoting Fares

  • 4.1.1 Domestic Flights and Journeys: For domestic travel within South Africa, fares are generally calculated based on distance and a fixed rate per kilometer. Additional costs include VAT, airport taxes, and sometimes service fees. Students should understand how to:
    • Identify fare zones or distance brackets
    • Apply correct rates per kilometer (e.g., R1.50/km)
    • Calculate total cost: Total Fare = Base Fare + VAT + Tax
    Example: A flight from Johannesburg to Durban (600 km) at R1.50/km = R900 base fare + 15% VAT = R1,035 total.
  • 4.1.2 Regional Flights and Journeys: These involve travel between South Africa and neighboring countries (e.g., Namibia, Botswana, Zimbabwe). Calculations follow similar methods but may include:
    • International departure tax
    • Currency exchange rate impacts
    • Possible interline agreements for connecting flights
    Example: Johannesburg to Gaborone (Botswana) flight ‚Äì 300 km √ó R1.80/km + international tax (R200) + fuel surcharge.

4.2 Recommending a Fare and/or Booking Class Based on a Fare Table, the Rules, and a Client Profile:

Travel consultants must analyze fare tables to recommend the most suitable booking class (e.g., Economy, Business, First) and fare type (refundable, non-refundable, flexible). Factors include:

  • Travel dates and times

Unit 5: General Rules on Reservations

5.1 IATA Recommendations in the Code of Reservation Ethics

  • 5.1.1 Booking Space: Travel agents must ensure that bookings made are genuine and based on a client‚Äôs confirmed intent to travel. Agents must avoid making duplicate or speculative bookings that may block availability for other travelers. Accurate passenger details and itinerary must be recorded at the time of booking.
  • 5.1.2 Cancelling Space: When a passenger cancels or changes their travel plans, the agent is responsible for immediately cancelling the reservation to free the seat for other passengers. Delayed cancellation may result in penalties or loss of reputation with the airline.
  • 5.1.3 Responsibility of Travel Agents: Agents are expected to act ethically by providing correct information, avoiding false reservations, and following standard procedures. They are responsible for keeping clients informed about changes, requirements, and fare conditions.

5.2 Rule 1‚Äì8 of IATA Resolution 830d (Reservation Procedures):

These are global procedures outlined by IATA for consistent and ethical booking practices. The rules include: using proper names, avoiding dummy bookings, advising clients of ticketing time limits, recording contact details correctly, and ensuring prompt cancellation of unused space. Failure to follow these rules can lead to financial penalties or loss of agency accreditation.

5.3 Applying the Rules and Recommendations:

Travel consultants must apply IATA‚Äôs rules and ethical recommendations in their daily work. They should ensure compliance with airline systems, avoid overbooking, and always communicate clearly with clients. Maintaining professionalism in all bookings helps build client trust and avoids unnecessary complications.

üìò Exam Tip

Unit 6: How to Make a Reservation

6.1 Making a Telephone Reservation:

When making a telephone reservation, travel consultants must follow a professional and structured approach:

  • Greet the client politely and identify yourself and your agency.
  • Gather all necessary travel details: full names (as per ID/passport), destination, travel dates, preferred airline/class, and any special needs (e.g., wheelchair access or meal preferences).
  • Repeat the details back to confirm accuracy.
  • Confirm availability and provide options for flights, fares, and booking classes.
  • Advise the client of ticketing time limits and any applicable conditions (e.g., refund policies, visa requirements).
  • Record all booking details clearly and ensure client contact details are captured accurately.
Communication should be clear, courteous, and efficient.

6.2 Giving a Written Account of a Telephone Reservation:

After making a reservation by phone, it is important to document the conversation in writing. This includes:

  • Client‚Äôs name and contact information
  • Flight details (airline, flight number, date, time, route)
  • Fare quoted and booking class
  • Special requests or notes (e.g., seat preference, meal choice)
  • Reservation reference number (PNR) and ticketing deadline
The written summary should be professional, dated, and filed or sent to the client as confirmation.

üìò Exam Tip

Know the full step-by-step process of making a telephone reservation and how to summarise it clearly in writing. You may be asked in exams to write a sample telephone booking log or identify missing information from a booking scenario. Accuracy, clarity, and professionalism are key!

Unit 7: Documentation

7.1 Selecting Transport, Accommodation, and Tours, and Completing a Client File

Travel consultants must select appropriate travel services that match the client‚Äôs preferences, needs, and budget. This includes:

  • Transport: Choosing air, rail, coach, or car hire based on routes, fares, and travel times.
  • Accommodation: Matching client preferences with star rating, location, and amenities.
  • Tours: Selecting suitable guided tours or excursions based on interests and available time.
A completed client file must contain:
  • Client‚Äôs personal details (name, ID/passport, contact info)
  • Confirmed itinerary (dates, times, and providers)
  • Payment and invoicing records
  • Travel insurance details (if applicable)
  • All required documents ‚Äì vouchers, tickets, confirmations

7.2 Completing Booking Documents

  • 7.2.1 Hotel Voucher (Domestic Booking): A hotel voucher is a document provided to the client and presented at the accommodation. It should include:
    • Guest name(s)
    • Hotel name, address, and contact number
    • Check-in and check-out dates
    • Room type and meal plan (e.g., B&B, half-board)
    • Booking reference number
    • Payment details (e.g., pre-paid, pay at hotel)
    • Special notes (e.g., early check-in, late checkout)
  • Booking Files: A booking file is the central record of all travel arrangements. It must be completed accurately and updated regularly. The file includes:
    • Client profile
    • Flight and transport bookings
    • Accommodation reservations
    • Tour or activity bookings
    • Copies of all communication with client and service providers
    • Confirmation numbers and payment records

üìò Exam Tip

Be familiar with the structure and content of both a hotel voucher and a full booking file. In exams, you may be asked to complete or correct a client booking document. Practice identifying missing or incorrect information in typical booking forms and understand what a complete client file should look like.
