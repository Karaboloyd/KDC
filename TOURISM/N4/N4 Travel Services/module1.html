Unit 1: Travel Industry Terminology

This unit introduces key terms and concepts commonly used in the tourism and travel industry. These terminologies are essential for understanding the functioning of travel agencies, tour operations, transportation services, and destination management. Students are advised to learn these terms and their definitions thoroughly, as they form the foundation of the tourism industry.

  • Tourism: The activities of people traveling to and staying in places outside their usual environment for leisure, business, or other purposes.
  • Tourist: A person who travels to a place for pleasure and stays at least 24 hours but not more than one consecutive year.
  • Excursionist (Day Visitor): A visitor who does not spend the night in a destination.
  • Inbound Tourism: When non-residents travel into a country.
  • Outbound Tourism: When residents of a country travel to another country.
  • Domestic Tourism: When residents of a country travel within their own country.
  • Travel Agent: A person or company that arranges and sells travel and accommodation services to clients.
  • Tour Operator: A company that designs, prepares, and sells tour packages, often combining transport, accommodation, and excursions.
  • GDS (Global Distribution System): A computerized network system used to book airline seats, hotel rooms, car rentals, and more.
  • IATA (International Air Transport Association): The trade association of the world‚Äôs airlines, promoting safe and economical air travel.

Unit 2: Components of the Tourism Industry

2.1 Components

  • 2.1.1 Producers (Principals): These are the main service providers in the tourism industry and include:
    • Carriers: Airlines, cruise lines, buses, and car rental companies that provide transport for tourists.
    • Accommodation: Hotels, motels, guesthouses, B&Bs, and lodges that offer overnight lodging and hospitality services.
    • Attractions: Natural (mountains, wildlife, beaches) and man-made (theme parks, museums, heritage sites) points of interest that motivate tourists to visit a destination.
  • 2.1.2 Support Services: These include both public and private sector services that support tourism development and operations. Examples:
    • Public entities: Government departments like the Department of Tourism, SA Tourism, Home Affairs (for visas/passports), and police services (for tourist safety).
    • Private entities: Travel insurance companies, banks (foreign exchange), restaurants, event organizers, IT companies (booking systems), and marketing agencies.

2.2 Role Players in the Travel Industry

  • 2.2.1 Principals: These are the direct providers of tourism services such as airlines, hotels, and attraction sites. They deliver the core travel experience.
  • 2.2.2 Tour Operators: Also called wholesalers, they package tourism products by combining accommodation, transport, and activities into a tour package. They sell packages directly to the public or through travel agents.
  • 2.2.3 Travel Agents / Consultants: Intermediaries who sell travel services to clients. They provide advice, make reservations, offer quotations, and issue travel documents.
  • 2.3 Principals and Tour Operators (Wholesalers): Principals provide the services (transport, accommodation), while wholesalers/tour operators combine these into ready-made packages for sale. They often negotiate better prices due to bulk buying.
  • 2.4 Functions of a Travel Consultant:
    • Advising clients on travel options and destinations
    • Making bookings and reservations (flights, accommodation, tours)
    • Providing quotations and pricing information
    • Issuing travel documents like tickets, itineraries, and invoices
    • Providing after-sales service and handling complaints or changes

üìò Exam Tip

Clearly understand the difference between producers/principals, tour operators, and travel consultants. Be able to give examples and explain their roles. Also practice short answers about support services and the value chain of tourism.

Unit 3: Governing Tourism Bodies & Other Organisations

  • 3.1 Role of South African Tourism Authority (SAT): SAT is responsible for marketing South Africa as a tourist destination both locally and internationally.
    • Objectives: To grow the tourism sector and increase tourist arrivals.
    • Mission: To promote sustainable tourism growth and improve the economic contribution of tourism to South Africa.
    • Functions: Market research, global promotion, hosting tourism expos (e.g., Indaba), and partnerships with stakeholders.
  • 3.2 Tourism Organisations in South Africa:
    • DEAT: Department of Environmental Affairs and Tourism ‚Äì Responsible for policy and regulation of tourism development.
    • SATSA: Southern Africa Tourism Services Association ‚Äì Promotes professionalism and accountability among tourism operators.
    • THETA: Tourism, Hospitality & Sport Education and Training Authority ‚Äì Develops skills and training for the tourism workforce.
    • Tourism Grading Council of South Africa (TGCSA): Responsible for grading accommodation establishments (e.g., star ratings).
    • South African Airways (SAA): National carrier that contributes to inbound tourism and job creation.
    • FEDHASA: Federated Hospitality Association of South Africa ‚Äì Represents the interests of hotels and accommodation providers.
    • Tourism Business Council of South Africa (TBCSA): Umbrella organization representing the business interests of tourism stakeholders.

üìò Exam Tip

Know the full names, roles, and functions of each tourism body. SAT, TGCSA, and DEAT are often asked in multiple-choice or short-answer format. Also review differences between regulatory bodies and marketing organizations.
