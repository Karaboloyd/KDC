Unit 1: Passports

1.1 Definition of Passports (IATA)

According to IATA, a passport is an official travel document issued by a government that certifies the holder‚Äôs identity and nationality. It is required for international travel and includes the traveller‚Äôs name, photo, date of birth, and signature.

1.2 Documents That Could Be Used in Lieu of a Passport

In specific cases, alternative documents may be accepted for travel, such as:

  • Emergency Travel Certificate (ETC) ‚Äì issued by South African missions abroad if a passport is lost or stolen.
  • Temporary Passport ‚Äì used for urgent travel within the SADC region when regular passports are unavailable.
  • National ID Cards ‚Äì accepted in some regional travel zones (e.g., within the EU or SADC in the future).

1.3 Conditions for Issuing a Joint Passport

A joint passport is a document where a parent/guardian and one or more minor children are included in the same passport. It is now largely phased out in favour of individual passports for each traveller. Joint passports may still be used in some older documents but are no longer issued in South Africa.

1.4 How and Where to Obtain Passports

South African passports are issued by the Department of Home Affairs. Applicants must submit the following:

  • Completed passport application form (DHA-73)
  • Valid South African ID
  • Two colour passport photos (depending on office requirements)
  • Proof of payment of application fee

Applications can be submitted at a Home Affairs branch or via online booking with selected banks offering eHomeAffairs services.

1.5 Validity of South African Passports

The validity of passports depends on the type:

  • Regular passport: Valid for 10 years (adults)
  • Child passport: Valid for 5 years
  • Temporary passport: Valid for limited periods (usually up to 12 months)

It is recommended that a passport has at least 6 months‚Äô validity when travelling internationally.

Unit 2: Visas

2.1 Definition of Visas and Transit Visas (IATA)

A visa is an official endorsement or stamp placed in a traveller's passport by the immigration authorities of a country, granting permission to enter, stay, or transit through that country for a specified period and purpose. Visas can be issued for tourism, business, study, work, or transit purposes.

A transit visa is a special visa granted to travellers who pass through a country en route to another destination. It usually allows short stays (from a few hours up to several days) in the transit area or within the country, depending on the immigration laws.

2.2 Visa Requirements, Validity, and Where to Obtain Visas

Visa requirements vary depending on the traveller‚Äôs nationality, the destination country, and the purpose of the visit. Some countries allow visa-free travel or visa-on-arrival for certain nationals.

  • To obtain a visa, travellers generally need to apply at the embassy or consulate of the destination country, providing documents such as a valid passport, completed visa application forms, photographs, proof of travel arrangements, financial means, and sometimes a letter of invitation.
  • Visas have specific validity periods, which define how long the holder can stay in the country. Some visas are single-entry, allowing one entry only, while others may be multiple-entry, permitting several entries during the validity period.
  • Electronic visas (e-visas) and online applications are increasingly common and can be obtained via official government websites.

Unit 3: Permits

3.1 Regulations Published in the Government Gazette of South Africa (GSA)

The regulations concerning permits for specific items when travelling are published officially in the Government Gazette of South Africa (GSA). These regulations specify which goods require permits for export, import, or transit through South Africa and other countries. Items such as firearms, antiques, cultural artefacts, certain plants and animals, and restricted medicines must have appropriate permits in accordance with national and international laws.

3.2 Advising Prospective Tourists on Permits for Specific Items When Entering Foreign Countries

Travel consultants must inform prospective tourists about the need for permits when carrying specific items into foreign countries. These may include:

  • Firearms and ammunition, which require import/export permits and must comply with strict regulations.
  • Protected wildlife, plants, or animal products that may require CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species) permits.
  • High-value electronic equipment or professional gear that may require temporary import permits.
  • Certain medicines or controlled substances that require medical prescriptions and permits.

Advising clients properly helps avoid confiscation of items at customs and ensures compliance with international travel laws.

üìò Exam Tip

Be familiar with the types of items that require permits and the legal sources where these regulations are published (such as the GSA). Understand the role of travel consultants in advising clients about permits, especially for restricted or controlled goods. Exam questions may include identifying items requiring permits or explaining the consequences of non-compliance.

Unit 4: Health Documents

4.1 Organisation Responsible for Standardisation of International Health Certificates

The World Health Organization (WHO) is the international body responsible for the standardisation and regulation of health certificates used in international travel. WHO sets the guidelines and formats for health certificates and vaccination records to ensure consistency and global recognition.

4.2 Certificate Requirements

  • 4.2.1 International Health Certificates: These documents certify the health status of a traveller, often required when entering countries with specific health risks. They include information on vaccination history, recent medical examinations, and any relevant health conditions.
  • 4.2.2 Certificates of Vaccination: Also known as ‚ÄúYellow Cards‚Äù or the International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis (ICVP), this certificate records vaccinations such as yellow fever, polio, and meningitis, which may be mandatory for entry into some countries.
  • 4.2.3 Validity of Certificates of Vaccination: The validity period varies depending on the vaccine. For example, yellow fever certificates are valid for 10 years from the date of vaccination. It is essential that travellers ensure their certificates are current and meet destination country requirements.

4.3 Interpreting Information from Countries Requiring Vaccination

Travel consultants must understand the vaccination requirements of destination countries, including which vaccinations are compulsory or recommended. Some countries require proof of vaccination upon arrival, especially those where diseases such as yellow fever are endemic.

4.4 Regulations Regarding the Validity of Vaccinations

Vaccinations must be administered by authorized medical personnel and documented properly. Vaccines administered outside the validity period may not be accepted, potentially leading to quarantine or denial of entry. It is the traveller‚Äôs responsibility to keep vaccination records updated.

üìò Exam Tip

Students should know the role of WHO in health certificate standardisation and be able to identify the types of health documents required for international travel. Be clear on the validity periods of common vaccination certificates and how to interpret country-specific vaccination requirements. Exam questions often include case studies where students must advise travellers on health documentation.

Unit 5: Documentation

5.1 Forms to be Used for Passports, Visas, and Permits According to Their Codes

Various official forms are used in the application and processing of passports, visas, and permits. These forms are coded for easy identification and reference:

  • 5.1.1 Passport Application Forms: In South Africa, the most common form is the DHA-73, which is used to apply for a new passport or renew an existing one. It includes sections for personal details, citizenship status, and emergency contacts.
  • Visa Application Forms: These vary depending on the country of destination, but typically require personal information, travel itinerary, purpose of visit, and proof of financial means.
  • Permit Application Forms: Specific forms are used for permits such as import/export of restricted goods, firearms, or special travel-related permits, each with their own codes and requirements.

5.2 Application Forms for Passports and Visas

Completing application forms accurately is crucial. Application forms generally request the following information:

  • Full name as per identity document
  • Date and place of birth
  • Nationality and citizenship details
  • Contact information and address
  • Purpose of travel and duration
  • Payment details and declarations

Below is an example of a typical passport application form layout showing the required fields (this can be shown as a table or image in learning materials):

5.3 Identifying Errors on Completed Forms

Common errors include incomplete sections, incorrect dates, mismatched names, illegible handwriting, missing signatures, or failure to attach required documents. Travel consultants must carefully review forms to avoid delays or rejection.

Ensuring accuracy prevents processing issues and assists travellers in obtaining their travel documents on time.
