Unit 1: Airport Facilities

1.1 Facilities Available to Passengers

  • 1.1.1 International and Domestic Departures:

    Airports provide clearly marked zones for international and domestic travel. Facilities typically include check-in counters, immigration, customs control (for international), boarding gates, and baggage claim areas. Major South African airports include:

    • OR Tambo International (IATA Code: JNB)
    • Cape Town International (IATA Code: CPT)
    • King Shaka International (IATA Code: DUR)
  • 1.1.2 Business and Economy Class Passengers:

    Different classes offer different levels of service:

    • Business Class: Priority check-in, extra baggage allowance, access to exclusive lounges, more legroom, onboard meals, and faster boarding.
    • Economy Class: Standard seating, limited baggage allowance, and basic meal options. Usually more affordable for general passengers.
  • 1.1.3 Passengers Who Need Special Care:

    These passengers include the elderly, people with disabilities, unaccompanied minors, and pregnant women. Services provided:

    • Wheelchair assistance
    • Priority boarding
    • Escort services for minors
    • Pre-arranged medical assistance

    Passengers requiring special care should indicate this when booking. Airlines follow strict guidelines (ICAO standards) for their handling and safety.

  • 1.1.4 Current Facilities at Airports:

    Modern airports provide various facilities to improve the travel experience:

    • Automated check-in kiosks
    • Wi-Fi access zones
    • Duty-free shopping and restaurants
    • Car rental desks
    • Medical and lost-and-found services
    • Prayer rooms and family rest areas

1.2 Pictograms and Illustrations of Airport Facilities

Airports use standardized symbols (pictograms) to help travelers navigate. These include icons for toilets, baggage claim, elevators, first aid, immigration, arrivals, departures, check-in, and boarding gates. Students should familiarize themselves with these symbols for both exams and practical use.

1.3 Facilities That Can Be Pre-Booked

Passengers can pre-book several airport-related services to make their journey more convenient. These include:

  • Wheelchair assistance or special-needs services
  • Airport lounges (paid or included with business class)
  • Car parking services
  • Car hire services
  • Hotel accommodation or airport shuttles

Be familiar with airport symbols (pictograms) as these may be shown in exams with multiple-choice options.

Unit 3: Baggage Requirements

3.1 IATA Definitions and Terminology

Define the following:

  • 3.1.1 Baggage: All items and belongings carried by a passenger while travelling. It includes both checked-in and carry-on items.
  • 3.1.2 Checked Baggage: Baggage handed over at check-in to be stored in the aircraft's cargo hold. It is tagged and retrieved at the destination.
  • 3.1.3 Unchecked Baggage (Carry-on Items): Also called hand luggage, these are items passengers take into the cabin. Size and weight are strictly limited for security and safety reasons.
  • 3.1.4 Free Baggage Allowance: The amount of baggage (by weight or number of pieces) that passengers can carry without paying additional fees, determined by airline and route.

3.2 Weight and Piece Concepts

Airlines apply one of two baggage policies:

  • Weight Concept: Baggage is limited by total weight (e.g., 23 kg or 30 kg). Common on international flights (excluding North America).
  • Piece Concept: Passengers are allowed a fixed number of bags (e.g., 2 pieces, each not exceeding 23 kg). Common for travel to/from North America.

3.3 Free Baggage Allowance (Weight Concept)

  • 3.3.1 International Travel: Most airlines allow 20‚Äì30 kg per economy class passenger. Business class passengers may receive more.
  • 3.3.2 Domestic Travel: South African domestic flights typically allow 20 kg in economy class. LCCs (low-cost carriers) may charge separately.
  • 3.3.3 Exceptions to Normal Baggage: Infants may be allowed a pram or car seat. Sporting equipment and musical instruments may count as excess baggage.
  • 3.3.4 Carry-On Items: Usually limited to one item not exceeding 7‚Äì10 kg, plus one small personal item (e.g., handbag or laptop).
  • 3.3.5 Items Not Allowed in Checked Baggage: Fragile items, valuable items (e.g., jewelry, electronics), cash, medicine, and documents must be carried as hand luggage.
  • 3.3.6 Dangerous Goods: Items such as explosives, flammable liquids, gases, toxic substances, and corrosives are prohibited. Refer to ICAO and IATA lists for classification.

3.4 Free Baggage Allowance (Piece Concept)

  • 3.4.1 Restrictions on Number of Pieces and Dimensions: Typically, each piece must not exceed 23 kg and total dimensions (length + width + height) must not exceed 158 cm.
  • 3.4.2 Children and Infants: Infants not occupying a seat are usually allowed 1 piece of baggage and 1 stroller or car seat free of charge.
  • 3.4.3 Special Articles: Items like sports gear or musical instruments may be accepted but require prior arrangements and may incur additional charges.

3.5 Baggage Shipped as Cargo

Baggage exceeding size/weight limits or shipped separately must be declared and transported as cargo. Cargo shipments require special documentation and fees.

3.6 Items, Weight, Size, and Number of Baggage Allowed

  • 3.6.1 Carry-On Baggage (Cabin): Typically 1 bag per passenger, 7‚Äì10 kg, with size not exceeding 56 x 36 x 23 cm.
  • 3.6.2 Checked Baggage: Weight allowance usually ranges from 20‚Äì32 kg depending on class and airline. Size is limited to 158 cm in total dimensions.
  • 3.6.3 Items Carried as Cargo Only: Oversized, hazardous, or live animals are transported in the cargo hold under specific airline policies.

3.7 Rules on Pooling Baggage

Pooling refers to combining baggage allowances between passengers. Some airlines allow pooling for passengers on the same reservation; others do not. Always check airline policies beforehand.

Review IATA baggage definitions and know examples of restricted or dangerous goods. This section often appears as multiple-choice, match-the-columns, or short-answer questions.

Unit 4: Check-In Procedures

4.1 Minimum Reporting Times

  • 4.1.1 Domestic Flights: Passengers are advised to arrive at the airport at least 90 minutes before the scheduled departure time. Check-in counters generally close 30‚Äì45 minutes before departure, depending on the airline.
  • 4.1.2 International Flights: Passengers should report to the airport at least 3 hours prior to departure. This allows enough time for check-in, security checks, passport control, and boarding.

4.2 Check-In for Onward and Return Journeys Simultaneously

Many airlines allow passengers to check in for both onward and return flights if they are operated by the same airline or partner carriers. This helps reduce delays and waiting time at transit or return check-in counters. E-tickets and itinerary details must be presented at the initial check-in counter.

4.3 Correct Sequence When Advising a Passenger About Check-In Procedures

When advising a client about the check-in process, travel consultants must follow this sequence:

  1. Advise the minimum reporting time based on flight type (domestic or international).
  2. Inform the passenger to bring all necessary travel documents (passport, visa, ticket/booking confirmation, and ID).
  3. Explain baggage allowance rules (including weight, number of items, and restrictions).
  4. Advise on special services if needed (e.g., wheelchair, unaccompanied minors).
  5. Notify the passenger to proceed to the airline‚Äôs check-in desk or self-service kiosk on arrival.
  6. Remind them to collect a boarding pass and proceed through security well in advance.

üìò Exam Tip

Know the exact reporting times for both domestic and international flights. Be able to explain the steps of the check-in process in the correct sequence. Common exam questions ask students to complete missing steps or place steps in logical order. Review the differences between check-in at the airport and online check-in options.

Unit 5: Customs Regulations

5.1 Procedures for Departing Passengers

  • 5.1.1 Boarding International Flights: Passengers must proceed to immigration after check-in. A valid passport, visa (if applicable), and boarding pass are required. Customs may ask questions about items being taken abroad, especially high-value items.
  • 5.1.2 Boarding Cruise Liners: Similar to international air travel. Passengers must present ID/passport and cruise tickets. Customs officers may inspect luggage. Ports have designated areas for customs and immigration processing.
  • 5.1.3 Registration of Valuables: South African citizens travelling abroad should declare and register high-value items (e.g., cameras, laptops, jewelry) with customs before departure. A DA65 form may be issued to prove items were owned prior to departure.

5.2 Passengers Arriving on International Flights

  • 5.2.1 Foreigners: Required to present a valid passport and visa (if applicable). Customs may check luggage, and immigration verifies the purpose of visit. Entry stamps and biometric scans are usually taken.
  • 5.2.2 South Africans: Must present a valid passport. Customs may ask to declare goods exceeding duty-free limits. Personal effects are usually allowed duty-free.

5.3 Customs Regulations

  • 5.3.1 Duty-Free Allowance: Passengers are allowed to bring goods into the country without paying customs duties up to a certain value. As of the current rules:
    • Alcohol: 2 litres of wine and 1 litre of spirits
    • Cigarettes: 200
    • Perfume: 50 ml
    • General goods: Up to R5,000
  • 5.3.2 Flat-Rate Assessments: For goods exceeding the duty-free limit (but within R20,000), a flat 20% duty may be charged instead of full tax and VAT.
  • 5.3.3 Moving Through the Green Channel: Used when a passenger has nothing to declare. If stopped by an officer and undeclared goods are found, fines and confiscation may apply.
  • 5.3.4 Currency Control: South Africans must declare any amount over R25,000 (or its equivalent in foreign currency). Foreign travellers must declare currency over US$10,000. Failure to declare may lead to confiscation or prosecution.
  • 5.3.5 Items Requiring Permits: These include firearms, live animals, plants, medicines, and cultural artefacts. Permits must be obtained before travel, and items must be declared at customs.

5.4 Procedures for Reclaiming Goods

If goods are confiscated or temporarily detained by customs, the passenger may apply for their return by submitting documentation (e.g., proof of purchase, permits). Claims must be made within a specified time frame at the customs office with the receipt or seizure notice.

üìò Exam Tip

Learn the duty-free limits and what qualifies for a flat-rate assessment. Know the difference between the green and red channels. Understand what must be declared or registered. Common exam questions include matching items with procedures and identifying when a permit is required.

Unit 6: Transiting Passengers

6.1 Concept and Basic Conditions

A transiting passenger is one who passes through an airport en route to a final destination. They do not leave the airport or enter the country.

  • Must have a valid ticket and visa for the next leg of the journey.
  • Transit visa may be required depending on the country‚Äôs immigration laws.
  • In most airports, transiting passengers remain in the transit lounge.

6.2 Passengers with Different Disabilities

Airports and airlines are required to assist passengers with physical, hearing, visual, or cognitive disabilities. Services may include:

  • Wheelchair assistance from curb to gate
  • Boarding support and pre-boarding priority
  • Special seating arrangements and onboard assistance
  • Communication assistance (e.g., sign language interpreters or written instructions)

Travel consultants must notify the airline in advance when booking for passengers with disabilities to ensure all support services are arranged.

üìò Exam Tip

Understand who qualifies as a transit passenger and when a transit visa is required. Also review the services airlines must provide for passengers with disabilities. This may be tested with scenarios or multiple-choice questions asking how to assist special-needs travellers.

Module 2 Summary: Passenger Services

This module introduces students to the key services and procedures involved in passenger handling within the travel and tourism industry. It begins with an overview of airport facilities, distinguishing between international and domestic services, business and economy class, and special services for passengers with disabilities. The module also explores important baggage regulations including definitions, allowance concepts (weight and piece), prohibited items, and the rules for shipping baggage as cargo. Students are taught the correct procedures for check-in, including minimum reporting times, onward and return journey check-ins, and how to advise clients. Customs regulations are also covered in detail‚Äîfocusing on duty-free allowances, declaration procedures, and special permits for items. Finally, the module explains the handling of transiting passengers and the facilities and support provided for travellers with special needs.

A strong understanding of passenger services helps students provide accurate advice to clients, comply with legal requirements, and enhance the overall travel experience for passengers. For exams, students must be able to compare domestic vs. international procedures, understand baggage and customs rules, and demonstrate the correct sequence of passenger check-in and support services. Real-life scenarios and definitions are often tested through multiple-choice, matching, and case-study questions.
