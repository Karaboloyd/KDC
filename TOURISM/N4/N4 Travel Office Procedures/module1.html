Unit 1: Travel Offices

1.1 Types of Travel Offices

  • Leisure Travel Agency: Focuses on selling holiday packages, cruises, and flights to the general public. These agencies specialize in leisure and recreational travel.
    Examples: Flight Centre, Thompson Holidays
  • Corporate Travel Agency: Caters to businesses by handling employee travel arrangements such as flights, accommodation, and meetings.
    Examples: HRG Rennies Travel, BCD Travel
  • In-House Travel Agency: Operates within a company to manage internal travel needs, mainly for employees.
    Examples: Vodacom in-house travel unit, Anglo American corporate travel

1.1.2 Wholesalers in Tourism

  • Inbound Tour Operator: Organizes and provides travel services to international tourists visiting the operator's country. They create local tour packages.
    Example: South African inbound operator offering Kruger Park safari tours to European tourists
  • Outbound Tour Operator: Arranges travel for local residents to international destinations, creating packages for travel abroad.

1.2 Departments in a Travel Office

  • Sales Department: Sells travel packages and services to clients
  • Reservations Department: Handles bookings for flights, hotels, and tours
  • Finance Department: Manages payments, invoicing, and budgets
  • Marketing Department: Promotes the agency's services to attract clients
  • Human Resources: Recruits and supports staff within the travel office

Duties of Each Staff Member

  • Manager: Oversees the entire travel office operations, sets goals, ensures compliance, supervises staff, and approves financial and operational decisions.
  • Finance Manager: Manages the financial records, budgets, invoicing, and ensures the agency's accounts and client payments are accurate and up to date.
  • Senior Travel Consultant: Handles high-profile clients, manages bookings and quotations, supervises junior staff, and provides expert advice on complex travel arrangements.
  • Intermediate Consultant: Assists clients with standard bookings such as flights, car hire, and hotels. Provides support to senior consultants and handles client inquiries.
  • Junior Consultant: Performs basic travel booking tasks under supervision. Learns agency processes, handles data entry, and supports more experienced consultants.
  • Receptionist: Welcomes clients, manages the front desk, handles phone calls, responds to general inquiries, and directs customers to appropriate departments.
  • Admin Clerk: Maintains records, files documents, prepares reports, supports data processing, and helps with paperwork related to bookings and payments.
  • General Worker: Assists with office cleanliness, errands, deliveries, refreshments, and basic maintenance duties to support a clean and safe work environment.

Unit 2: The Office

2.1 Location

Location plays a crucial role in the success of tourism establishments. When choosing a location, several factors must be considered to ensure sustainability, profitability, and accessibility. Below are key considerations, in line with the DHET Report 191 syllabus:

  • Demand of the product: A high demand ensures consistent income. Before opening, research if tourists or local clients need your service. Visit the location and speak with the community or potential clients. For example, an area with high tourist traffic may have demand for local guided tours.
  • Availability of labour: Ensure that skilled or trainable workers are available nearby. If your business needs tour guides, receptionists, or drivers, the area should have people willing and able to do those jobs without requiring costly relocation.
  • Transport routes: The location should be easy to reach via road, taxi, or even rail. If tourists or staff can‚Äôt access your office easily, it will affect your business. Choose areas close to major roads or public transport routes.
  • Market of the product (clients): Consider whether your target market lives or travels through that area. For example, opening a travel agency in a corporate office park is suitable for business travel clients, but not for adventure tourists.
  • Services (power, sanitation, water): Your location must have reliable services. Load-shedding, water shortages, or poor sanitation can harm operations and reduce customer satisfaction.
  • Competitors: Too many similar businesses nearby can affect your profits. However, a few competitors might mean demand exists. Assess the balance‚Äîare you bringing something unique?

2. Equipment

Equipment in a modern travel office includes:

  • Computers and laptops with internet access
  • Telephones and headsets
  • Printers and photocopiers
  • Scanners
  • Filing cabinets or digital storage systems
  • Office desks and chairs
  • Software for bookings and reservations

3. Lighting

Offices should have natural light and well-placed artificial lighting. Poor lighting can lead to eye strain and reduce productivity. Fluorescent and LED lighting are commonly used.

4. Noise

Offices must be sound-managed to avoid distractions. Use carpeting, acoustic panels, or noise machines. In areas requiring focus (e.g. finance), noise should be minimal.

5. Warming and Ventilation

The office should be well-ventilated with fresh air and temperature control. Air conditioners, heaters, and openable windows help create a comfortable working environment.

6. Security

Protecting employees and company property is essential. Security features include:

  • 6A. Burglar bars on doors/windows
  • 6B. Alarm systems
  • 6C. Panic buttons
  • 6D. CCTV cameras
  • 6E. Security guards
  • 6F. Safe, designated parking
  • 6G. Computer and system passwords
  • 6H. Access control cards

Unit 3: Mail

3.1 Traditional Mail

Traditional mail refers to the physical transmission of documents, parcels, and letters through various mailing systems. Each service has its own nature (how it operates) and importance (why it is useful in a travel office). Let‚Äôs discuss these below:

3.1.1 Explain the nature and importance of the following:

  • 1A. Speed Services

    • Explanation: Speed services are the fastest form of traditional mail. They are used when urgency or priority delivery is required. The sender pays a normal postage fee plus an additional express delivery fee depending on the type of service selected.

    Examples:

    • Counter to Counter: Item is sent and collected at the post office.
    • Counter to Door: Item is posted at the counter and delivered to the recipient‚Äôs address.
    • Door to Counter: Item is collected from the sender and the recipient collects it at the post office.
    • Door to Door: Collected from the sender and delivered directly to the recipient.

    Nature:

    • Operates within a short delivery timeframe (usually overnight or same day).
    • Ideal for urgent documents or packages.

    Importance:

    • Provides fast and reliable delivery for urgent office correspondence.
    • Helps avoid delays in critical operations (e.g., travel bookings, confirmations).
  • 1B. Registered Mail

    • Explanation: Registered mail is a secured postal service where the item is recorded throughout the mailing process. It is used for sending valuable or sensitive items such as contracts, legal papers, or money orders.

    Example: Sending a signed contract or confidential document with proof of delivery and tracking.

    Nature:

    • Each item is tracked during delivery.
    • Recipient must sign upon receipt.
    • Sender receives a receipt and tracking number.

    Importance:

    • Provides security and accountability.
    • Prevents loss and theft.
    • Offers proof of delivery, useful in disputes or verification.
  • 1C. Docex (Document Exchange)

    • Explanation: Docex is a private document exchange service used mostly by legal firms, travel agencies, and professional businesses. It allows for the daily exchange of sensitive documents between registered members.

    Example: A travel agency sending client contracts or payment confirmations to a tourism board using Docex bags.

    Nature:

    • Operates within a closed network between registered members only.
    • Not handled by the Post Office but by the Docex service provider.
    • Daily pickups and deliveries to registered addresses.

    Importance:

    • Ensures safe and quick delivery of confidential documents.
    • Reduces delays, especially within the same city or business hub.
    • Popular in professional and tourism sectors.
  • 1D. Regular/Surface Mail

    • Explanation: This is the most basic form of postal service, used for non-urgent items such as brochures or newsletters.

    Example: Sending travel brochures, newsletters, or statements to clients.

    Nature:

    • Low cost but slow delivery (can take days or weeks).
    • No tracking or insurance.
    • Sent via road, rail, or sea.

    Importance:

    • Cost-effective for bulk or non-urgent mail.
    • Good for marketing or informational material.
  • 1E. Courier Services

    • Explanation: Courier services are private delivery companies that offer fast, insured, and trackable door-to-door services.

    Examples: DHL, FedEx, Aramex, The Courier Guy

    Nature:

    • Offers tracking, insurance, and reliable delivery (local and international).
    • Pickup and delivery at the client's address.

    Importance:

    • Essential for urgent and high-value deliveries.
    • Improves professional image and client confidence.

3.1.2 Electronic Mail

Explanation: Electronic mail (email) is a method of sending and receiving messages, documents, and digital files through the internet.

Emails are commonly used in travel offices for sending itineraries, confirmations, quotes, invoices, and communicating with clients or suppliers.

Example: A travel agent sending a quotation and itinerary to a client via email.

Nature:

  • Fast and instant delivery
  • Supports file attachments (e.g. PDFs, images, Word docs)
  • Accessible from computers and mobile devices
  • Can be formal or informal

Importance:

  • Saves time and postage costs
  • Improves communication and client service
  • Environmentally friendly (paperless)
  • Keeps a record of communication
  • Enables quick responses and file sharing

3.3 Procedures for Opening and Recording Mail

Traditional Mail:

  • Collect mail from the post box or mailroom
  • Sort by department, urgency, type
  • Check for damage
  • Open carefully using a letter opener
  • Date-stamp received mail
  • Record in the incoming mail register with details

Electronic Mail:

  • Check inbox regularly
  • Classify emails: urgent, internal, client, etc.
  • Reply or forward appropriately
  • Organise using folders or labels
  • Print and file critical attachments

3.4 Systems of Distributing Mail

  • Distributing Incoming Mail:

    • All incoming traditional and electronic mail must be delivered quickly to the correct staff or department. Use pigeonholes, internal envelopes, or forwarding via email.

    Mark urgent mail clearly and use internal delivery times (e.g. 10:00 AM, 2:00 PM).

  • Collecting Outgoing Mail:
    • Make copies of documents
    • Mark special instructions (e.g. ‚ÄúSpeed Mail‚Äù)
    • Check that all enclosures are included
    • Verify recipient address and code
    • Record mail in an outgoing register
    • Mail must be dispatched at least once daily
    • Place all mail in the outgoing tray for collection

Unit 4: Confidentiality

4.1 What is Confidentiality?

Confidentiality refers to the responsibility of a tourism establishment to protect sensitive information from being accessed, shared, or used by unauthorized individuals. In the context of a travel and tourism office, confidentiality ensures that all personal, financial, and operational data of clients, employees, and suppliers is kept private and secure. Breaching confidentiality can lead to loss of client trust, legal consequences, and damage to the reputation of the business.

4.2 Types of Confidential Information

The following types of information are considered confidential and must be protected in a tourism establishment:

  • Full name and surname of clients and staff members
  • Identification numbers (e.g. ID numbers or passport numbers)
  • Bank account information
  • Credit card details
  • Home or residential addresses of clients and staff
  • Client itineraries and travel schedules
  • Contracts or agreements with suppliers
  • Travel insurance documents
  • Visa application details
  • Client contact details (email addresses and phone numbers)
  • Customer databases or mailing lists
  • Expiry dates of official travel documents (e.g. passports, visas)

This type of information, if disclosed improperly, can lead to identity theft, fraud, or misuse of business data. It is the duty of all staff to handle this information with care.

4.3 Access to Confidential Information

Not every staff member in a tourism establishment should have access to confidential information. Access must be restricted based on job roles and responsibilities. The following staff members are usually allowed access:

  • Manager or Supervisor: Oversees all operations and may require access to staff records, contracts, and client bookings for decision-making purposes.
  • Travel Consultant or Agent: Needs access to client travel documents, payment information, and booking details to manage travel plans.
  • Finance or Accounts Officer: Handles invoices, contracts, and financial records including bank and credit card information.
  • Human Resources (HR): Manages employee contracts, ID numbers, and personal data of staff members.
  • IT Administrator: Maintains secure storage of digital files and databases but must follow access control protocols.

4.4 Company Procedure

4.4.1 Examples of Company Procedures Relating to Confidentiality

Tourism establishments put procedures in place to protect confidential information and manage any misconduct. Common examples include:

  • All staff must sign confidentiality agreements upon employment.
  • Passwords and access codes must be kept private and changed regularly.
  • Confidential files should be locked or encrypted when not in use.
  • Only staff with authorized roles may access sensitive data.
  • Employees are prohibited from discussing private information with outsiders or unauthorized staff.

When these procedures are violated, disciplinary action is taken. Depending on the seriousness of the offense, consequences may include:

  1. Verbal warning for first-time or minor infractions
  2. Written warning placed in the employee's file
  3. Final written warning if repeated or more serious misconduct occurs
  4. A monetary fine or salary deduction (in some organizations)
  5. Termination of contract or dismissal for severe or repeated violations

4.4.2 Reporting Violations According to Company Procedures

Any suspected breach of confidentiality should be reported immediately using the correct channels. The typical steps are:

  1. The incident is reported verbally or in writing to a supervisor or manager.
  2. A formal report is completed, documenting the event and individuals involved.
  3. Management investigates the matter to assess the severity and potential risk.
  4. Appropriate action is taken based on the organization's policies and code of conduct.
  5. Clients or stakeholders affected by the breach may be notified if necessary.

Violations of confidentiality may include:

  • Accessing files or systems without proper authorization
  • Sharing login details or passwords with other people
  • Using another person‚Äôs private information for personal gain
  • Providing sensitive information to external companies or individuals

Organizations must ensure all staff are trained in confidentiality policies and understand the consequences of any violations.

Unit 5: Storage of Information

5.1 Classifying, Sorting, and Recording Paper-Based Documentation

In a tourism establishment, it is essential to manage all office records in a way that ensures information is easy to locate, secure, and available when needed. Paper-based documents must be classified, sorted, and recorded according to the company‚Äôs filing and documentation procedures.

Proper storage allows for accurate retrieval of information, supports smooth office operations, and protects documents from damage or loss.

Common examples of paper-based records in a travel office include:

  • Client booking forms
  • Supplier contracts
  • Invoices and receipts
  • Travel insurance documents
  • Staff records and correspondence

Steps for Handling Paper-Based Documentation:

  1. Classify: Group documents based on type, purpose, or department (e.g., finance, HR, client files).
  2. Sort: Arrange the documents in a logical sequence, such as by date, name, client reference number, or alphabetical order.
  3. Record: Register the documents in a manual or digital logbook for easy tracking. This ensures that each document has a reference point in the filing system.
  4. File: Store documents in cabinets, folders, or binders with clear labels.

5.1.1 Methods of Classification and Cross-Referencing

  • Alphabetical Filing: Based on client or company name.
  • Numerical Filing: Using reference or file numbers.
  • Chronological Filing: Sorted by date.
  • Subject Filing: Grouped by theme or service type.
  • Geographical Filing: Based on physical location or region.

Cross-Referencing:

Cross-referencing helps locate documents stored under different categories. It prevents duplication and confusion. For example, if a file is under a surname, a reference under the client's first name or booking type may help others find it quickly.

5.1.2 Problems from Misclassification or Incorrect Cross-Referencing

If classification or cross-referencing is done incorrectly, it can lead to several problems:

  • Lower staff productivity due to wasted time searching for files
  • Inaccurate information retrieval
  • Loss or duplication of documents
  • Client service delays
  • Damage to professional reputation
  • Increased stress or confusion among employees

5.1.3 Storing Information in the Correct Location and Sequence

To store documents correctly, follow a logical and systematic approach:

  • Use labeled folders or dividers for categories (e.g., "Air Bookings", "Accommodation")
  • Place documents in order by client name, number, or date
  • Ensure filing cabinets and shelves are clearly marked
  • Keep daily logs of files added or removed
  • Update filing systems regularly to prevent backlog

5.1.4 Storing Documents to Ensure Safety, Accessibility, and Efficiency

A proper filing system must meet both company and staff needs. It should be:

  • Suitable: Matches the nature of tourism business operations
  • Logical: Information is easy to trace using consistent methods
  • Flexible: Can adapt to changes in volume or structure
  • Simple: Easy for all staff to understand and use
  • Accessible: Quick to reach during busy periods
  • Economic: Cost-effective with minimal waste of resources
  • Safe: Prevents unauthorized access or accidental loss
  • Durable: Materials used should last over time (e.g., strong folders, fireproof cabinets)
  • Neat: Maintains a clean, professional office environment
  • Compact: Uses space efficiently without clutter

5.1.5 Effects of Misfiled Documents on Productivity

Misfiled records in a tourism business can negatively affect productivity in the following ways:

  • Wasted time trying to find missing documents
  • Delays in confirming client bookings or processing payments
  • Increased likelihood of errors or double-bookings
  • Loss of client confidence due to poor service
  • Disruptions in daily office tasks

To maintain efficiency, staff must be trained on proper filing methods and follow company procedures at all times.

5.1.6 Completing Filing Using Company Policy

Filing must be done daily or weekly, depending on the volume of documents. Each file must be labelled clearly using standard formats (e.g., "CLIENT-BOOKINGS-2025"). Staff must use proper filing cabinets or locked drawers to ensure confidentiality, especially for client and financial information.

5.1.7 Locating and Dispatching Information

When information is requested:

  1. Search using the appropriate classification system.
  2. Obtain a copy of the required document.
  3. Ensure confidentiality by placing the document in an envelope or emailing securely.
  4. Send it to the correct department, staff member, or external recipient as required.

5.1.8 Tracking Retired Information

Retired files (no longer in regular use) must be recorded in a logbook or spreadsheet with:

  • File name or number
  • Date retired
  • Location of archived file (e.g., archive room or storage box ID)
  • Access details (who archived it and who can retrieve it)

5.1.9 Identifying and Locating Missing Items

Missing or overdue files can be identified when:

  • The file is not found in its correct place.
  • A file has not been returned by the due date (tracked using a sign-out sheet or digital log).

Procedures to follow:

  1. Check with the last person who signed out the file.
  2. Search backup systems (cross-referenced logs or copies).
  3. Inform a supervisor if the file cannot be found within 24 hours.
  4. Log the incident and consider creating a replacement if required.

5.2 Electronic Filing

5.2.1 Creating and Labelling Electronic Files

Files are created on a secure system (e.g., internal server, cloud storage, or company software). Folders must be clearly labelled using standard naming conventions such as:

  • CLIENTS_2025_JUNE
  • SUPPLIER_CONTRACTS_Q1
  • STAFF_RECORDS

5.2.2 Classifying, Sorting, and Recording Electronic Documents

Digital files should be:

  • Organised by folders (e.g., per client, per tour package).
  • Sorted by document type (e.g., invoices, itineraries, personal IDs).
  • Backed up regularly to prevent data loss.
  • Recorded in an index or digital tracking system, with access control to ensure only authorised users can view or modify documents.
